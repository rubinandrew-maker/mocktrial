Home / Case 1: Hazing

State v. UEC Soccer Team

Criminal Hazing · California Education Code § 32050
Why This Matters in Real Life

Hazing is a serious problem on college campuses across the country — and it causes real harm. California's hazing law exists because many hazing traditions don't look dangerous on the surface, but create enormous social pressure that leads students to take risks they never would have taken otherwise. This case asks: when does a fun team tradition become a crime?

Background: University of El Cajon

The University of El Cajon (UEC) is a mid-sized public university in El Cajon, California. Its mascot is the Great Owl, and the campus is known for its mature oak and eucalyptus trees. The men's and women's soccer programs are highly competitive.

For at least six years, new players joining the UEC soccer team have participated in a tradition tied to the team's first match. Returning players informally told new recruits that each newcomer was expected to bring a "good luck charm" — something "unique and impressive" — specifically, "a selfie from a place no one has ever been or would expect to see a selfie from."

Returning players shared past examples in the team group chat, including selfies from a rooftop reached by climbing a locked ladder, and from inside a locked campus mechanical room. New players were never explicitly told what would happen if they didn't participate — but socially, those with impressive charms were celebrated, and those without were subtly excluded and passed to less during early games.

There is no written record of this tradition. Head Coach Renata Villanueva said she was unaware that it involved any physical risk.

What Happened: Freddy Suares

Freddy Suares is an 18-year-old freshman from San Diego on a soccer scholarship. About two weeks before the first match, returning players showed Freddy and two other freshmen a large owl nest in a eucalyptus tree near the science building. Freddy thought it would make an extraordinary backdrop for his good luck charm selfie.

On September 14th, Freddy climbed the tree alone. He had checked — and saw no adult owl. He reached 18 feet and stood on a large branch near the nest. As he repositioned for a better camera angle, the branch snapped. Both Freddy and the branch fell. The branch struck the nest, sending it — and two owl chicks — to the ground. The chicks did not survive.

Freddy suffered a displaced forearm fracture and multiple bruises. He was taken by ambulance to the hospital.

The Charge: California Education Code § 32050
The Law in Plain English

California's hazing law says it's a crime to participate in any initiation activity — even an informal tradition — that causes or is likely to cause serious physical injury to a student trying to join a team or organization. The law applies even if no one explicitly ordered the student to do something dangerous.

What the Prosecution Must Prove

The prosecution must prove ALL of these beyond a reasonable doubt:

  • Hazing occurred: The "good luck charm" tradition was a form of initiation or pre-initiation into the soccer team.
  • Serious bodily injury: The tradition caused or was likely to cause serious injury (Freddy's broken arm qualifies).
  • Connected to membership: Participating was tied to being accepted on the team.
  • Consent is irrelevant: Under California law, it does NOT matter if Freddy "agreed" to participate — consent is not a defense to hazing.
Critical Legal Point: Consent Is NOT a Defense

California's hazing statute explicitly says that a victim choosing to participate does not let the organizers off the hook. This is a major point both sides must address. The defense cannot simply say "Freddy chose to do it."

What the Defense Will Argue
  • The tradition was voluntary and fun — no one ever told Freddy to climb a tree or do anything dangerous.
  • Freddy's choice to climb 18 feet into a tree was entirely his own idea — far beyond what any player had ever done before.
  • The team captains had no knowledge that anyone would take a dangerous risk.
  • The tradition itself — bringing a personal "charm" — is not inherently dangerous.
  • Coach Villanueva ran an anti-hazing program; this was not a rogue, unsupervised team.

Home / Case 2: MBTA

The People v. Freddy Suares

Migratory Bird Treaty Act · 16 U.S.C. § 703 & CA Fish & Game Code § 86
Why This Matters in Real Life

The Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA) is one of the oldest wildlife protection laws in the United States — passed in 1918. It protects over 1,000 species of birds. But courts across the country disagree about a big question: does the law only punish people who mean to harm birds, or does it apply to anyone whose actions kill a protected bird — even accidentally? This case puts Freddy at the center of that debate.

Freddy's Perspective

Approximately two weeks before the first match, a group of returning players showed Freddy and two other freshmen a large owl nest in a eucalyptus tree near the science building. The players pointed it out with admiration and called it a campus landmark.

Freddy immediately thought it would make the perfect backdrop for his "good luck charm" selfie. He did not tell anyone what he was planning. He did not research the owl species. He did not contact facilities staff or wildlife authorities.

On September 14th, Freddy returned to the tree alone in the early evening. He watched for several minutes, saw no adult owl, and concluded the nest was empty. He began climbing, reached 18 feet, and positioned himself on a large branch. As he adjusted for a better angle, the branch snapped. Both he and the branch fell — the branch struck the nest, sending it and two owl chicks to the ground. The chicks did not survive.

A U.S. Fish and Wildlife investigation confirmed the chicks were California Spotted Owls (Strix occidentalis occidentalis), a subspecies protected under the MBTA. Freddy never touched the nest or the birds. He had no idea this species was federally protected.

The Charges
Federal: MBTA
16 U.S.C. § 703 — Two counts (one per chick)

It is unlawful to pursue, hunt, take, capture, kill, or possess any migratory bird, or any part, nest, or egg of such bird. The California Spotted Owl is a covered species.

State: CA Fish & Game
California Fish and Game Code § 86

California's state law prohibits the "take" of protected wildlife species, which includes any action that results in the killing, harming, pursuing, or capturing of a protected animal.

What the Prosecution Must Prove
  • Freddy "took" the birds: His actions led to the death of two California Spotted Owl chicks, even if he didn't touch them.
  • Protected species: The California Spotted Owl is covered by the MBTA — a fact Agent Rivera can confirm.
  • Intent (contested): For a felony, some courts require knowing intent. The prosecution may argue that Freddy's approach to a nest he knew was a "California Spotted Owl nest" satisfies this — or that strict liability applies regardless.
What the Defense Will Argue
  • Freddy never intended to harm the birds — his only goal was a selfie with the nest in the background.
  • He had no knowledge of the MBTA or that this owl species was federally protected.
  • The branch was structurally damaged by prior storm damage — the real cause of the fall was the university's failure to inspect the tree.
  • The MBTA was designed to prevent commercial and industrial bird killing, not to criminalize individual accidents.
  • Applying strict liability here would make criminals out of millions of people who accidentally harm wildlife during everyday activities.
The Central Legal Dispute: Strict Liability vs. Knowing Intent
Prosecution's View (Strict Liability)

The plain text of the MBTA says it's unlawful to "take" a migratory bird — period. Intent doesn't matter. If your actions caused the death, you're liable. This makes the law consistent and easy to enforce.

Defense's View (Intent Required)

Federal courts are split. Several circuit courts have ruled that criminal prosecution under the MBTA requires at least some knowing or reckless conduct. Criminalizing all accidents sets a dangerous precedent.

How a Trial Works

From the first arrest to the final verdict — step by step
The Big Picture

A criminal trial is the government's attempt to prove — beyond a reasonable doubt — that a specific person committed a specific crime. The defendant is presumed innocent until proven guilty. It's the prosecution's job to overcome that presumption; it is never the defendant's job to prove their innocence.

The Trial Timeline
Before Trial
Arrest, Arraignment & Plea
The defendant is formally charged and appears in court. They enter a plea — Guilty, Not Guilty, or No Contest. If they plead not guilty, the case moves toward trial. Attorneys exchange evidence through a process called discovery.
Step 1
Jury Selection (Voir Dire)
Attorneys and the judge question potential jurors to find 12 (or fewer) people who can be fair and impartial. Each side can remove jurors they feel are biased through challenges. Voir dire is French for "to speak the truth" — jurors take an oath to answer honestly.
Step 2
Opening Statements
Both attorneys give an overview of the case. The prosecution goes first. Opening statements are not evidence — they're a roadmap. Attorneys tell the jury what they expect the evidence will show. This is the first impression that matters!
Step 3
Prosecution's Case
The prosecution presents its witnesses and evidence. For each witness: the prosecution does direct examination (asking their own witness questions), then the defense can do cross-examination (challenging the witness). Evidence like photos or documents must be formally introduced through a witness who can authenticate them.
Step 4
Defense's Case
The defense presents its witnesses and evidence. The defendant has the right to testify but is not required to — and the jury cannot hold silence against them. The prosecution can cross-examine defense witnesses. The defense may also call the same witnesses back to clarify their testimony.
Step 5
Closing Arguments
Both attorneys summarize the evidence and argue why the jury should find in their favor. The prosecution goes first, then the defense, then the prosecution gets a final rebuttal. Closing arguments are the most persuasive moment of the trial — this is where great attorneys shine.
Step 6
Jury Instructions
The judge reads the jury specific legal instructions explaining exactly what they must find — and what standard they must use — to convict or acquit. In criminal cases, the standard is beyond a reasonable doubt — the highest standard in American law.
Step 7
Deliberation & Verdict
The jury leaves the courtroom to deliberate privately. They review the evidence, discuss each charge, and must reach a unanimous verdict (all 12 must agree). They return a verdict of Guilty or Not Guilty for each charge. Not Guilty means the prosecution failed to meet its burden — not necessarily that the defendant is innocent.
How Evidence & Objections Work
Introducing Evidence
  • Attorney asks the judge to mark the item (e.g., "Your Honor, I'd like to mark this as Exhibit A")
  • Attorney shows it to opposing counsel
  • Attorney lays "foundation" through the witness — asking questions that show the item is authentic and relevant
  • Attorney moves to admit the exhibit: "Your Honor, I move to admit Exhibit A into evidence"
  • Judge admits it — now the jury can consider it
Making Objections
  • Stand up and say "Objection, Your Honor" followed by a brief reason
  • The judge rules: "Sustained" (objection granted — question is blocked) or "Overruled" (objection denied — witness must answer)
  • Common grounds: Relevance, Hearsay, Leading, Foundation, Speculation, Calls for a legal conclusion
  • Objections must be timely — raise them before the witness answers!

Roles & Responsibilities

Everyone in the courtroom has a specific job — know yours inside and out

Judge

The neutral ruler of the courtroom — your word is law
What You Do
  • Run the courtroom and maintain order
  • Rule on all objections (Sustained or Overruled)
  • Read jury instructions before deliberation
  • Decide on procedural issues (can a witness be recalled? Can an exhibit be shown?)
What You Wear

A black robe if available. Otherwise, professional attire in dark colors. You sit elevated above all other participants.

Sample Language
Objection sustained — the question calls for hearsay. Counselor, please rephrase.
Order. We will proceed. Counselor for the defense, you may call your next witness.
DO
  • Stay completely neutral — never signal which side you favor
  • Speak clearly and with authority
  • Ask attorneys to clarify if an objection is unclear
DON'T
  • Show frustration or favor to either side
  • Interrupt unnecessarily — let attorneys complete their questions
  • Help attorneys with their arguments

Prosecuting Attorney

You represent the People — prove the case beyond a reasonable doubt
What You Do
  • Deliver an opening statement laying out the evidence you'll present
  • Question your witnesses (direct examination)
  • Cross-examine defense witnesses to challenge their credibility
  • Introduce exhibits into evidence
  • Deliver a closing argument — and get a final rebuttal
Sample Language
Your Honor, I'd like to move to admit Prosecution Exhibit A into evidence.
Objection, Your Honor — the question is leading.
No further questions, Your Honor.
DO
  • Know every element of each charge cold
  • Use leading questions on cross-examination (allowed!)
  • Tie all evidence back to the specific legal elements
DON'T
  • Use leading questions on direct examination
  • State personal opinions ("I believe the defendant...")
  • Ask questions you don't know the answer to on cross

Defense Attorney

Your job is to create reasonable doubt — you don't have to prove innocence
What You Do
  • Cross-examine prosecution witnesses to find inconsistencies and weaknesses
  • Call and examine your own witnesses
  • Object to improper questions and evidence
  • Deliver a powerful closing argument focused on reasonable doubt
Sample Language
Objection, Your Honor — assumes facts not in evidence.
Your Honor, the defense moves to admit Defense Exhibit J, the arborist's report.
Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, the prosecution has not proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt.
DO
  • Focus on what the prosecution FAILED to prove
  • Use short, pointed questions on cross-examination
  • Remind the jury of the presumption of innocence
DON'T
  • Argue with the judge after a ruling
  • Let prosecution witnesses ramble — cut off non-responsive answers
  • Promise evidence in opening that you can't deliver

Witness

Tell your story truthfully and stay consistent under pressure
What You Do
  • Take an oath to tell the truth before testifying
  • Answer questions from your attorney (direct examination) with full, clear answers
  • Answer questions from the opposing attorney (cross-examination) honestly but carefully
  • Stay in character and only know what your character would know
Sample Language
To the best of my knowledge, yes — I saw that message in the group chat.
I'm not sure I understand the question. Could you please rephrase?
I don't recall the exact words, but my understanding was...
DO
  • Listen to every question carefully before answering
  • Say "I don't know" or "I don't remember" if that's true
  • Stay calm — don't let cross-examination rattle you
DON'T
  • Answer a question before your attorney can object
  • Volunteer information you weren't asked
  • Argue with the cross-examining attorney

Jury Member

The most important role in the room — you decide the verdict
What You Do
  • Listen to all testimony and evidence with an open mind
  • Take notes (if permitted) on key evidence and credibility of witnesses
  • Do NOT discuss the case with anyone during the trial
  • Deliberate with fellow jurors after all evidence is in
  • Reach a unanimous verdict on each charge
During Deliberation
  • Apply the jury instructions from the judge — what does the law actually require?
  • Ask: did the prosecution prove EVERY element of the crime beyond a reasonable doubt?
  • Evaluate witness credibility — who was consistent? Who had reasons to lie?
  • "Reasonable doubt" means honest, substantial doubt based on evidence — not just any possibility
DO
  • Keep an open mind throughout — don't decide early
  • Ask to have jury instructions re-read if confused
  • Base your verdict ONLY on evidence presented in court
DON'T
  • Let your personal feelings about the defendant affect you
  • Consider anything outside of the evidence presented
  • Cave to peer pressure — stand by your honest assessment

Bailiff

Maintain order and assist the judge and jury
What You Do
  • Call the court to order: "All rise! The Honorable [Name] presiding."
  • Administer the oath to witnesses: "Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?"
  • Escort the jury in and out of the courtroom
  • Maintain order in the gallery
Sample Language
All rise! The Superior Court of El Cajon County is now in session, the Honorable [Name] presiding. Please be seated.

Court Reporter

Record every word — the official record of the trial
What You Do
  • Record everything said during the trial — every question, answer, objection, and ruling
  • Ask speakers to slow down or repeat if needed
  • Keep the official transcript of the proceeding
Sample Language
Your Honor, could counsel please speak up? I couldn't hear the witness's last response.

Legal Glossary

30+ terms — click any term to expand its definition, or search below
Actus Reus
Actus reus is Latin for "guilty act." It refers to the physical action — or sometimes inaction — that makes up part of a crime. In our case, the actus reus for the hazing charge is the tradition itself; for the MBTA charge, it's Freddy climbing the tree and causing the nest to fall.
The prosecution must prove both that Freddy did something (actus reus) AND that he had the right mental state (mens rea) — in most crimes, both are required.
Mens Rea
Mens rea is Latin for "guilty mind." It refers to the intent or mental state required to commit a crime. Different crimes require different levels: purposeful, knowing, reckless, or negligent.
The big debate in Case 2 is whether the MBTA requires mens rea at all — or whether it imposes strict liability regardless of Freddy's intent.
Beyond a Reasonable Doubt
This is the highest standard of proof in American law — used in ALL criminal cases. It means the jury must be firmly convinced of the defendant's guilt. It is NOT "beyond any possible doubt," but it IS much more than "probably guilty." If a juror has honest, substantial doubt based on the evidence, they must vote Not Guilty.
If a juror thinks "Freddy probably did it, but I'm not really sure," that's reasonable doubt — they should vote Not Guilty.
Strict Liability
A legal standard where someone is responsible for harm regardless of their intent or mental state. You don't have to mean to do something wrong — just doing it is enough. Strict liability is rare in criminal law but is argued here under the MBTA.
Speed limits are strict liability: it doesn't matter if you were distracted or didn't notice the sign — if you were going 45 in a 35 zone, you're liable.
Objection
A formal protest made by an attorney during trial, asking the judge to disallow a question, answer, or piece of evidence because it violates the rules of evidence. Attorneys must state the legal ground for their objection (e.g., "objection, hearsay").
If a prosecutor asks a witness "What did your friend tell you the defendant said?" — the defense would object on grounds of hearsay.
Sustained
The judge's ruling that agrees with an objection. When an objection is sustained, the question is blocked — the witness may not answer it, and the jury is told to disregard it if it was already partially answered.
"Objection, leading!" / "Sustained. Counselor, please rephrase the question."
Overruled
The judge's ruling that rejects an objection. The question stands and the witness must answer it.
"Objection, relevance!" / "Overruled. The witness may answer."
Leading Question
A question that suggests the desired answer within the question itself. Leading questions are NOT allowed during direct examination (questioning your own witness) but ARE allowed during cross-examination.
Leading: "You were at the tree that night, weren't you?" / Non-leading: "Where were you on the night of September 14th?"
Hearsay
An out-of-court statement offered in court to prove the truth of what it says. Hearsay is generally not allowed because the person who actually made the statement isn't there to be cross-examined. There are many exceptions.
"My friend told me Freddy said he was going to climb the tree" — this is hearsay if offered to prove Freddy actually said it.
Foundation
The preliminary information an attorney must establish before a piece of evidence can be admitted or before an expert can testify. Foundation proves that the evidence is what it claims to be and that the witness has personal knowledge of it.
Before showing the jury the group chat screenshots (Exhibit A), the attorney must ask a witness to confirm they recognize the messages and that they are authentic.
Relevance
Evidence is relevant if it has a tendency to make a fact in the case more or less likely to be true. Only relevant evidence is admissible. "Objection, relevance" means the attorney believes the evidence or question has nothing to do with the case.
Freddy's GPA is probably irrelevant to whether he "took" a protected bird. His knowledge of the species of owl is very relevant.
Proximate Cause
The primary cause that, in a direct and unbroken sequence, led to the harm. To be convicted of a crime, the defendant's actions must be the proximate cause of the harm — not just a distant contributing factor.
Was it Freddy climbing the tree, or the university's failure to inspect the damaged branch, that was the proximate cause of the nest falling?
Superseding Cause
A new, independent event that breaks the chain of causation between the defendant's conduct and the harm — potentially reducing or eliminating liability. The defense in Case 2 argues the university's failure to inspect the storm-damaged tree is a superseding cause.
If a storm had struck the tree at the same moment Freddy was climbing and caused the branch to fall — that might be a superseding cause.
Hazing
Under California Education Code § 32050, hazing means any method of initiation or pre-initiation into a student organization that causes, or is likely to cause, serious bodily injury. Importantly, the victim's consent is NOT a defense. Hazing does not have to be explicit — implicit pressure counts.
A tradition that pressures new players to take risky selfies — even without a written rule — may qualify as hazing.
"Take" (MBTA Definition)
Under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, "take" means to pursue, hunt, shoot, wound, kill, trap, capture, or collect any migratory bird — or to attempt to do so. Courts have debated whether indirect actions that result in bird deaths also count as a "take."
Freddy never touched the birds — but his actions dislodged their nest and caused their deaths. Does that count as a "take"? That's the central question in Case 2.
Migratory Bird
A bird species protected under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act (1918). The Act covers over 1,000 species, including the California Spotted Owl. The law protects not just the birds, but also their nests, eggs, and parts.
The California Spotted Owl (Strix occidentalis occidentalis) is a covered migratory bird species, confirmed by Agent Rivera's field report (Exhibit C).
Cross-Examination
Questioning of a witness by the opposing attorney — the side that did NOT call that witness. Leading questions ARE allowed on cross-examination. The purpose is to challenge credibility, reveal inconsistencies, or elicit facts favorable to your side.
After the prosecution questions Agent Rivera directly, the defense attorney cross-examines him — challenging whether the MBTA has ever been used to prosecute individual accidents.
Direct Examination
Questioning of a witness by the attorney who called that witness. Leading questions are NOT allowed on direct examination. The goal is to tell your client's story through the witness in a clear, compelling way.
The prosecution conducts direct examination of Diego Mendes, asking him to describe — in his own words — the pressure he felt around the good luck charm tradition.
Voir Dire
The process of selecting a jury, where attorneys and the judge question potential jurors to identify and remove those who cannot be impartial. Voir dire is French for "to speak the truth." Each attorney can remove potential jurors using "for-cause" challenges (unlimited) or "peremptory" challenges (limited, no reason required).
An attorney might remove a juror during voir dire who says they already have a strong opinion about hazing cases.
Arraignment
The first formal court appearance after charges are filed. The defendant is read the charges against them and enters a plea (Guilty, Not Guilty, or No Contest). Bail is also often decided at this stage.
Freddy's arraignment would have been where he was formally told he is charged with two counts of violating the MBTA and one count under CA Fish & Game Code § 86.
Indictment
A formal accusation issued by a grand jury that there is enough evidence to charge someone with a serious crime. Not all cases require a grand jury indictment — prosecutors can sometimes file charges directly. An indictment is not a finding of guilt; it just means the case moves forward.
A grand jury might issue an indictment of Marco Delgado based on evidence in the group chat without him being present to defend himself.
Plea
The defendant's formal response to the charges: Guilty (admitting the crime), Not Guilty (denying guilt and demanding the prosecution prove its case), or No Contest (not admitting guilt but accepting the punishment — common in cases with related civil lawsuits).
Freddy enters a plea of Not Guilty — meaning this case goes to trial, and the prosecution must prove its case.
Burden of Proof
The obligation to prove the facts of your case. In criminal trials, the burden always rests with the prosecution — they must prove guilt. The defense has no burden to prove innocence. This directly reflects the presumption of innocence.
"The prosecution has the burden to prove, beyond a reasonable doubt, that Freddy Suares took a protected migratory bird."
Presumption of Innocence
The foundational rule of American criminal law: every defendant is presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt. The jury must begin deliberation assuming the defendant did NOT commit the crime — it's the prosecution's job to overcome that presumption.
Even if a juror thinks Freddy "probably did something wrong," that isn't enough. Presumption of innocence means they should vote Not Guilty unless the prosecution has proven every element.
Closing Argument
The final statement each attorney makes to the jury after all evidence has been presented. Closing arguments summarize the evidence, apply the law, and urge the jury to reach a specific verdict. This is NOT evidence — it is attorney argument. The prosecution goes first, then the defense, then the prosecution gets a rebuttal.
A strong closing: "The prosecution has not shown you that Freddy Suares knowingly took a protected bird. They've shown you a tragedy — and those are not the same thing."
Deliberation
The private discussion among jurors after all evidence and closing arguments are complete. Jurors deliberate until they reach a unanimous verdict on each charge. Everything said in deliberations is confidential. Jurors may ask the judge for clarification on legal instructions.
During deliberation, jurors might debate: "Does strict liability apply to the MBTA — or must the prosecution show that Freddy knew what he was doing?"
Verdict
The jury's final decision on each charge. In criminal cases, the verdict is either Guilty or Not Guilty for each charge. A verdict must be unanimous in most jurisdictions. The verdict is announced in open court by the foreperson.
"On the charge of violating the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, Count One — we the jury find the defendant... Not Guilty."
Acquittal
A Not Guilty verdict — the jury has determined the prosecution did not prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt. Once acquitted, a defendant CANNOT be tried again for the same crime (Double Jeopardy protection). Acquittal does not necessarily mean the defendant is factually innocent.
If the jury finds Freddy Not Guilty on the MBTA charges, he is acquitted — and the federal government cannot re-try him for the same incident.
Conviction
A Guilty verdict — the jury has determined the prosecution proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt. After a conviction, the judge determines the sentence (the punishment). The defendant may appeal a conviction to a higher court.
If the UEC Soccer Team is convicted of hazing, the judge would then sentence them — potentially including probation, fines, or community service.
Endangered Species / Protected Species
Species listed under the Endangered Species Act or other federal/state wildlife protection laws. "Protected species" is a broader category — a bird can be protected under the MBTA without being listed as endangered. The California Spotted Owl is listed as a "species of concern" and is protected under both federal and California law.
You don't have to be on the "endangered" list to be protected — the MBTA covers over 1,000 species, including many that are currently common.
Speculation
An objection raised when a question asks a witness to guess, assume, or infer something beyond their personal knowledge. Witnesses can only testify to things they personally know — not things they imagine might be true.
"What do you think Freddy was planning to do after he climbed the tree?" — objection, calls for speculation. The witness cannot know what Freddy was thinking.
Authentication
The process of proving that a piece of evidence is what it claims to be. Before the jury can see an exhibit, an attorney must present testimony or other evidence that the item is genuine.
Before Exhibit A (the group chat screenshots) can be shown to the jury, a witness must testify that those are real screenshots from the actual team chat — not fabrications.

Attorney Preparation Checklist
Common Objections Reference
Relevance
Use when: a question or evidence has nothing to do with the charges
Objection, Your Honor — relevance. This evidence is not material to any element of the charge.
Hearsay
Use when: a witness is testifying about what someone ELSE said, offered to prove the truth
Objection, Your Honor — hearsay. The witness is testifying about an out-of-court statement offered for its truth.
Leading Question
Use when: an attorney uses a yes/no question that suggests the answer on direct examination
Objection, Your Honor — the question is leading. Counsel is suggesting the answer to their own witness.
Lacks Foundation
Use when: a piece of evidence is introduced without proper authentication or background
Objection, Your Honor — lacks foundation. The witness has not established personal knowledge of this document.
Calls for Speculation
Use when: a question asks a witness to guess or infer beyond what they personally know
Objection, Your Honor — calls for speculation. The witness cannot know what another person was thinking.
Assumes Facts Not in Evidence
Use when: a question includes something as true that hasn't been proven yet
Objection, Your Honor — assumes facts not in evidence. The premise of that question has not been established.
Common Attorney Mistakes to Avoid
  • Asking questions you don't know the answer to on cross — you might open a door you can't close
  • Letting witnesses ramble — control the pace; you can redirect with "thank you, let me ask a more specific question..."
  • Arguing after an objection is overruled — accept the ruling and move on, or politely note your exception for the record
  • Making promises in opening you can't keep — if you say "the evidence will show X," make sure your witnesses actually establish X
  • Forgetting to move exhibits into evidence — you must formally admit them before the jury can consider them
  • Being too aggressive on cross — juries don't like attorneys who bully witnesses; be pointed, not cruel
Witness Preparation Checklist
Common Witness Mistakes to Avoid
  • Volunteering extra information — answer the question asked, then stop. Don't add "and also..."
  • Arguing with opposing counsel — answer questions calmly, even if the attorney is being aggressive
  • Changing your story — be consistent; if you're caught in a contradiction, acknowledge it calmly
  • Looking at your attorney for help — you must answer questions on your own during cross
  • Guessing — if you don't know, say so. Guessing wrong is worse than saying "I'm not sure."
Juror Preparation Checklist
Common Juror Mistakes to Avoid
  • Deciding too early — listen to ALL the evidence before forming a verdict
  • Basing your verdict on sympathy — feel for Freddy if you want, but base your vote on the law and the evidence
  • Caving to peer pressure — if you have real doubt, hold your ground. A unanimous verdict must be honest.
  • Confusing "probably guilty" with "proven guilty" — probably isn't enough. The standard is beyond a reasonable doubt.
  • Ignoring jury instructions — the judge's instructions are the law. Follow them even if you disagree personally.

How to Use This Page

These are the real questions the jury will wrestle with. For each one, we've laid out the strongest arguments on both sides. Use this page to prepare your arguments — or to anticipate what the other side will say.

Case 1: Hazing Questions
1
Does a tradition qualify as hazing even when no one explicitly ordered new members to participate?
Arguments FOR Hazing
  • Social pressure can be just as coercive as explicit orders
  • The group chat celebrated risky behavior and mocked "ordinary" charms
  • Expert: implicit traditions are recognized as the most coercive form of hazing
  • Freddy's playing time and friendships depended on participating
Arguments AGAINST Hazing
  • No one ever told Freddy to do anything dangerous
  • The statute says hazing "causes or is likely to cause" injury — was this tradition likely to cause injury?
  • Freddy's specific action was far beyond any prior example
  • The tradition had run for six years without any injury
2
Is consent a defense to hazing under California law — and if not, should it affect how we view the severity of the conduct?
Arguments FOR Conviction (Consent Is Irrelevant)
  • California Education Code § 32050 explicitly says consent is NOT a defense
  • Consent in hazing is never truly free when social belonging is at stake
  • The legislature chose to remove consent as a defense — courts should honor that choice
Arguments FOR Acquittal (Consent Still Matters)
  • Even if consent isn't a legal defense, it should affect the degree of moral culpability
  • Freddy was an adult who made a fully autonomous decision
  • Punishing team leaders for what a willing participant voluntarily chose raises fairness concerns
3
How much responsibility do team leaders bear for a tradition they didn't start and didn't explicitly endorse?
Arguments FOR Responsibility
  • Marco and Sofia are named captains — leadership comes with accountability
  • They were in the group chat and saw the risky selfie examples praised
  • They never corrected the tradition or made optionality explicit
  • The tradition was well-established enough to have six years of precedent
Arguments AGAINST Responsibility
  • Neither Marco nor Sofia created the tradition or gave instructions
  • Freddy never told anyone what he was planning
  • You can't be criminally responsible for what you didn't know and couldn't foresee
  • The tradition had never caused harm before — foreseeability of danger was low
4
Did the good luck charm tradition foreseeably incentivize dangerous behavior, even if dangerous behavior was never explicitly encouraged?
Yes — It Was Foreseeable
  • The group chat showed and celebrated multiple risky selfies
  • "Unique and impressive" + "somewhere no one would expect" logically leads toward risk
  • A reasonable person in Marco's position should have foreseen escalation
No — Not Foreseeable
  • Prior selfies showed safe but unusual locations — not 18 feet up a tree
  • Freddy's specific interpretation was far beyond any established norm
  • There is a difference between creative and dangerous
Case 2: MBTA Questions
5
Does the MBTA require knowing intent for criminal liability, or does it impose strict liability regardless of purpose?
Strict Liability Applies
  • The plain text says "it shall be unlawful" — no intent requirement mentioned
  • Congress chose not to include an intent element — courts should respect that
  • Protecting endangered birds is a compelling government interest that justifies strict liability
Intent Is Required
  • Federal courts are split — several circuits require knowing conduct for criminal prosecution
  • Criminal law traditionally requires mens rea — strict liability is the exception, not the rule
  • Applying strict liability here would criminalize anyone who accidentally kills a bird
  • The MBTA was written to target hunters and commercial interests, not individuals
6
Can a defendant "take" a protected bird by climbing near its nest if he never touched the bird or nest?
Yes — Indirect Actions Count
  • The MBTA broadly prohibits "pursue, hunt, take, capture, kill" — a wide spectrum of actions
  • The chicks died as a direct result of Freddy's choice to climb toward the nest
  • The law's purpose — protecting birds — would be undermined by a narrow "must touch the bird" reading
No — He Never Took Anything
  • Freddy never pursued, hunted, captured, or touched the birds
  • The branch failure was a freak accident — not an intentional act toward the nest
  • Reading "take" this broadly would create unlimited criminal exposure for anyone near wildlife
7
Does the weakened branch constitute a superseding cause that reduces or eliminates Freddy's criminal liability?
No — Freddy Remains the Proximate Cause
  • Freddy chose to climb a tree — the branch condition was a foreseeable risk
  • He didn't test the branch's stability before trusting it with his full weight
  • A superseding cause must be truly unforeseeable — a weak branch in a neglected tree is foreseeable
Yes — The University Bears Responsibility
  • The arborist found the branch was structurally compromised before Freddy arrived
  • The branch would have failed under ANY adult's weight — Freddy's presence wasn't the decisive factor
  • The university's failure to inspect the storm-damaged tree broke the causal chain
  • Freddy had no way to know the branch was internally decayed
8
Is it relevant that Freddy's purpose was a selfie — an act millions of people engage in near wildlife every day?
No — Purpose Doesn't Matter
  • The harm to the birds was the same regardless of Freddy's reason for climbing
  • If strict liability applies, the "why" is irrelevant by definition
  • People taking selfies near wildlife cause documented harm to protected species nationwide
Yes — Context Matters for Fairness
  • Criminalizing selfie behavior would expose millions of ordinary people to federal prosecution
  • The MBTA was designed for hunters and industrial actors, not photographers
  • Prosecutorial discretion should account for the absence of harmful intent
  • A conviction here would set an alarming precedent with no logical stopping point
